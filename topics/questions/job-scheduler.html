Questions

Design a Distributed Job Scheduler

Tests whether you can turn "run this job at this time" into a leader-election and exactly-once-dispatch problem instead of quietly relying on a single cron box.

45-55 min interview Hard
Say this in the first 2 minutes
  • The core insight: this is a leader-election + exactly-once-dispatch problem — cron on one box is a single point of failure, cron on every box double-fires every job
  • Key scale number to anchor on: 10M scheduled jobs with sub-minute recurrence granularity means the scheduler must scan and claim due jobs continuously, not just once a minute
  • Primary tradeoff being probed: at-least-once dispatch with idempotent workers vs true exactly-once — most production schedulers pick the former because it's dramatically simpler to build correctly
  • Component most candidates forget: a durable "next_run_time" index so a scheduler doesn't have to replay history to figure out what's due
  • Failure mode most candidates skip: what happens to missed jobs when the entire scheduler tier is down for an hour during a deploy or outage

Step 4 of 6 — Data Flow

Data flow

Dispatch is a write path (claim + enqueue, blue/amber) that races against no one else because of the claim; status lookups are a separate read path (mint) against the Execution log.

Job Store / Execution Log — durable Postgres, source of truth
Scheduler shard — the component doing the claiming
Queue + workers — decoupled async execution
  1. Scheduler shard scans for due jobs — queries its partition of the next_run_time index for rows due within the next lease window
  2. Shard claims each due job — conditional UPDATE sets claimed_by/claimed_until, only succeeds if unclaimed or lease expired
  3. Claimed job is enqueued — pushed onto the durable dispatch queue, decoupling claim success from actual execution
  4. Worker pulls and executes — runs the job payload, reports success/failure back to the Execution log
  5. next_run_time is advanced — for recurring jobs, computed from the cron expression and written back so the next scan picks it up correctly

Step 6 of 6 — Deep Dives

Deep dive: leader election and sharding

Jobs are partitioned across scheduler shards by hash(job_id) mod N. Each shard elects exactly one leader via the coordination service; only the leader scans and claims that shard's jobs.

Active leader — the only node scanning/claiming its shard
Coordination Svc / Job Store — durable truth both sides trust
Standby promoting after a detected leader failure
  1. Jobs are partitioned — hash(job_id) mod N assigns every job to exactly one shard, deterministically
  2. Each shard elects a leader — nodes race to acquire an ephemeral lock/lease in the coordination service; loser becomes standby
  3. Leader renews its lease continuously — a short TTL (a few seconds) means failure is detected fast without needing explicit heartCheck failure signals
  4. Leader crashes → lease lapses — the coordination service releases the lock, standby nodes race to acquire it
  5. New leader re-scans from the durable index — because next_run_time lives in the Job Store, not in the old leader's memory, no scheduling state is lost on failover
The key design choice: scheduler shards are stateless with respect to "what's due" — all of that lives in the durable Job Store's next_run_time index. A leader failing just means a brief pause in scanning that partition, never lost knowledge of what to run.

Deep dive: exactly-once dispatch via conditional claim

  • Failover can briefly produce two leaders (lapsed lease unnoticed).
  • Real guard: compare-and-swap claim on the job row itself.
Job row — the single point of truth the CAS operates on
Winning replica — proceeds to enqueue
Dispatch queue — receives exactly one enqueue for this job/run
  1. Both replicas find the same due job — a race is possible during failover windows or clock skew between shard scans
  2. Both issue a conditional UPDATE — SET claimed_by = self, claimed_until = now() + lease WHERE job_id = X AND claimed_until < now()
  3. Only one UPDATE matches the WHERE clause — the database's row-level locking guarantees exactly one writer sees an affected-rows count of 1
  4. The winner enqueues the job — exactly one dispatch reaches the queue for this run
  5. The loser detects 0 affected rows and moves on — no error, no retry storm, just proceeds to the next due job in its batch
This only works if the claim and the "is this job due" check happen against the same durable, transactional store. If next_run_time lived in a cache instead of Postgres, a stale cache read could let both replicas believe they won.

Deep dive: scheduler drift and catch-up

  • Extended scheduler outage → backlog of missed runs.
  • Durable next_run_time index means no full-history replay.
  • Catch-up is just a policy decision for what's overdue.
Job Store — one stale next_run_time value, not a log of every miss
Run-once-immediately — the sane default for most recurring jobs
Run-all-missed — expensive, only for jobs where every interval has meaning
  1. Scheduler tier goes down — no shard is scanning or claiming; due jobs simply accumulate against their existing next_run_time
  2. Nothing is lost, nothing is duplicated during the outage — because next_run_time is a single durable field per job, not an append-only log of every missed tick
  3. Tier comes back and resumes scanning — every job whose next_run_time is now in the past is "due," possibly by a large margin
  4. Catch-up policy decides what happens next — run-once-immediately (fire once, then resume normal cadence), skip-to-latest (recompute next_run_time strictly forward, don't fire for missed ticks), or run-all-missed (fire once per missed interval — expensive, rarely correct)
  5. Policy is set per job, not globally — a metrics-rollup cron job should skip-to-latest; a billing-cycle job may need run-all-missed
Run-all-missed is a trap for anything sub-minute: 40 minutes down on a 1-minute job means 40 backlogged executions firing in a burst the moment the tier recovers, which can itself take the system down again. Default to skip-to-latest or run-once-immediately unless a job explicitly needs every interval accounted for.

Interview moves

  • 01
    "This is fundamentally a leader-election and exactly-once-dispatch problem, not a cron-syntax problem" — states the core insight unprompted, before diving into scheduling algorithms
  • 02
    "I wouldn't run cron on one box — single point of failure — and I wouldn't run it on every box either, since that double-fires every job" — shows you understand why the naive approaches both fail
  • 03
    "The claim itself needs to be a conditional compare-and-swap on the job row — that's what survives two schedulers racing on the same due job" — names the specific mechanism, not just "use locking"
  • 04
    "A scheduler shard should be stateless about what's due — all of that lives in a durable next_run_time index, so a leader failing never loses scheduling state" — separates transient compute from durable truth
  • 05
    "I'd default to at-least-once dispatch with idempotent workers rather than chase true exactly-once — it's dramatically simpler and the failure mode is a safe retry, not silent loss" — demonstrates pragmatic tradeoff judgment
  • 06
    "After an extended outage I wouldn't run every missed interval by default — I'd pick a per-job catch-up policy, because run-all-missed can cause a dispatch storm the moment the system recovers" — proactively surfaces a second-order failure mode

Follow-up questions

How do you handle clock skew between scheduler nodes?
Use the database's own clock (e.g. Postgres now()) inside the claim's WHERE clause rather than each node's local clock — the CAS still only lets one writer win regardless of skew.
What happens if a worker claims a job but then dies before reporting status?
The execution row's claimed_until lease expires; a monitor requeues it as a retry attempt. Idempotent job design ensures a partial or duplicate run doesn't corrupt state.
How would you support 10M jobs without the scan becoming the bottleneck?
Shard the Job Store itself, keep a tight (next_run_time, claimed_until) composite index per shard, and scan in small time-windowed batches instead of scanning the full table.
Why not just use the coordination service (ZooKeeper/etcd) to store job state directly?
Coordination services are optimized for small, low-churn config/consensus data, not millions of rows with high write throughput — Postgres with proper indexing scales dispatch state far better.
How do you prevent a "thundering herd" when many jobs share the same schedule (e.g. hourly at :00)?
Jitter next_run_time by a small random offset per job at creation time, spreading dispatch load across a few seconds instead of one instant.
How would this design change to support Airflow-style task dependencies?
It wouldn't fit cleanly — that needs a DAG execution engine tracking upstream/downstream task state, which is a different problem layered on top of this dispatch primitive, not a variation of it.
