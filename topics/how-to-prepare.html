How to Prepare

The highest-ROI study strategy — what to learn, in what order, and how to practice.

8 min read Foundations
The 3 things that move the needle
  • Practice delivering out loud — reading is not the same as doing
  • Learn a delivery framework first; memorizing components without structure is useless
  • Study 10-15 canonical questions deeply, not 50 questions shallowly
  • Understand the WHY behind every technology choice, not just the WHAT
  • Time yourself — 45 min is shorter than you think

Mental model

Preparing for system design is like learning to drive: reading the manual helps, but the only thing that actually works is getting behind the wheel and narrating your decisions out loud.

The right practice loop

Set a 45 min timer
→
Deliver out loud
→
Review gaps
→
Repeat until smooth
  1. Pick a question: Start with URL Shortener (scope is well-defined, great for framework practice)
  2. Timer: Hard constraint — teaches you to manage the 5-phase structure under pressure
  3. Deliver out loud: Narrate as if there's an interviewer in the room; silence is a red flag in real interviews
  4. Review gaps: After the session, identify what you couldn't justify or didn't know
  5. Repeat: Same question until you can deliver it cleanly, then move to the next

High-ROI vs low-ROI prep

High ROI
  • Practicing delivery with a timer
  • Understanding tradeoffs for 5-6 key tech choices
  • Learning latency numbers by heart
  • Doing 1 mock interview per week with feedback
  • Studying Hello Interview question breakdowns deeply
Low ROI (avoid)
  • Reading without practicing delivery
  • Covering 50+ questions at surface level
  • Memorizing architecture diagrams without understanding why
  • Watching YouTube without pausing to answer yourself first
  • Skipping back-of-envelope estimation

Interview moves

  • 01
    Practice the framework before the question bank — one week on delivery structure pays off more than five weeks on memorized answers
  • 02
    Learn the "why" for 6 core tech choices — SQL vs NoSQL, Redis, Kafka, CDN, consistent hashing, and sharding cover 90% of what comes up
  • 03
    Study one question per 2 days, not one per hour — depth over breadth; interviewers probe, not quiz
  • 04
    Record yourself once — watching a 10-min clip of yourself is more valuable than 3 hours of reading

Common follow-up questions

How long does it realistically take to get interview-ready?
4 weeks for most engineers with some distributed systems exposure. 6-8 weeks if you're starting from scratch. The bottleneck is always practice reps, not content consumption.
Is Hello Interview enough, or do I need other resources?
Hello Interview covers everything you need. The only supplement worth adding is 1-2 mock interviews with a human (peer or paid service) for feedback on communication.
Should I study ML system design separately?
Yes, if targeting Nvidia or Apple ML roles. Same framework, different component vocabulary: feature store, model serving, training pipeline, embedding index. Study 3-4 ML-specific questions.
How do I know if I'm ready?
You can deliver any Tier 1 question cold in 40 minutes — with justifications for every choice, a bottleneck analysis, and 3 failure modes — without consulting notes.
What's the single highest-ROI thing to do in the last 48 hours before an interview?
Do one timed mock on a question you haven't practiced in a week. Refresh latency numbers. Don't cram new topics — solidify what you already know.
Does my production experience help?
Yes — significantly. Frame your Oracle observability experience (Prometheus/Grafana at 37k tenants) as scale evidence. Interviewers weight real production judgment over textbook answers.
