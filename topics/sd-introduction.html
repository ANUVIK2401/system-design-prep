What is a System Design Interview

What interviewers are actually evaluating — and what separates a pass from a hire.

10 min read Start here
The interviewer's mental checklist
  • Can you drive the conversation without being prompted at every step?
  • Do you clarify before designing — or do you jump straight to solutions?
  • Can you make decisions under ambiguity and defend them with tradeoffs?
  • Do you know what breaks at scale, and how to fix it?
  • Are you someone this team would trust to own a complex system?

Mental model

A system design interview is not a quiz — it's a 45-minute simulation of your first week on the job, where your manager says "we need to build X" and watches how you think.

There is no single correct answer. What's being evaluated is your engineering judgment: how you break down a vague problem, what questions you ask, which tradeoffs you reason about, and whether you can communicate a coherent design under time pressure.

Interview structure

Client / gateway
Cache — hot path
  1. Clarify: Nail down functional requirements, users, scale, and what you're NOT building
  2. Estimate: Back-of-envelope QPS, storage, and bandwidth — drives your component choices
  3. High-level design: Core components, APIs, data model, data flow
  4. Deep dive: Interviewer picks the hardest part; you go deep on bottlenecks and failure modes
  5. Wrap-up: Summarize tradeoffs, mention what you'd improve with more time

What trips candidates up

Strong candidates
  • Ask about scale before drawing anything
  • Explicitly state why they chose a technology
  • Surface tradeoffs without being asked
  • Drive the interview — don't wait for prompts
  • Say "I'd handle X like this, but there's a cost..."
Common failure modes
  • Jump to drawing components before clarifying scope
  • Name-drop tech without justifying the choice
  • Only talk about happy-path flows
  • Wait to be asked before mentioning a problem
  • Treat it like a coding problem with one right answer

Interview formats by company

✓
FAANG / MANGA (Google, Meta, Apple, Amazon, Netflix): 45-60 min, one problem, emphasis on scale and deep dives. Expect follow-ups on every decision.
⚠
Mid-size (Stripe, Airbnb, Uber, Databricks): Often more focused — may be a specific sub-problem. More emphasis on API design and data modeling.
⚠
Nvidia / Apple ML roles: May include ML system design (model serving, inference pipeline, feature store). Same framework, different components.

Interview moves

  • 01
    "Before I start, let me clarify a few things" — opens every interview; signals structured thinking immediately
  • 02
    "I'll assume 10M DAU unless you'd like a different scale" — shows you think in numbers, gives the interviewer a chance to redirect
  • 03
    "I'm going to start with the high-level and then we can double-click on any component" — frames the interview, shows you can manage time
  • 04
    "I chose X here. The tradeoff is Y — I'm comfortable with that given our scale" — every technology choice should sound like this
  • 05
    "The bottleneck here is [X]. Here's how I'd address it..." — say this before the interviewer has to ask; shows you see failure modes proactively

Common follow-up questions

How is a system design interview different from a coding interview?
No single correct answer. It tests ambiguity handling, breadth of knowledge, and communication — not algorithmic precision. You're evaluated on process and judgment, not output.
What if I don't know a specific technology?
Say so, and reason from first principles: "I haven't used Kafka directly, but I'd think about this as a durable message queue — here's what I'd need from it..." Interviewers care more about your thinking than your memorized tool list.
How much should I draw vs talk?
Draw the high-level architecture diagram early — it anchors the conversation. Narrate while drawing. Use the diagram to point to components when you deep-dive. Don't draw every detail upfront.
What if the interviewer keeps redirecting me?
They're telling you what they care about. Pivot immediately, go deep, then say "should I continue here or move on to X?" They're testing whether you can follow their lead.
How long should I spend on requirements?
5 minutes max. Cover: core features (2-3), excluded features (1-2), users, scale, and SLA. Then start designing. Over-spending on requirements is a red flag for time management.
Is there a minimum bar for knowing distributed systems theory?
Yes: CAP theorem, eventual vs strong consistency, and common failure modes. You don't need to cite papers — you need to reason correctly about tradeoffs when they come up.
